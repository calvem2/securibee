How to recognize email phishing attempts.

about

Adversaries often try to exploit folx by sending official looking emails to trick users into giving them funds or personal information.

what do we mean by "email phishing"?

Email phishing is a type of scam where adversaries pose as official organizations or someone you know (e.g. relatives, friends, or coworkers) to gain access to your sensitive personal information.

who can the adversary pretend to be?

Anyone that you would be likely to respond to if an urgent matter is posed:

  • Relative
  • Friend
  • Co-Worker
  • Boss
  • Company (e.g. Apple, Amazon)
  • Law enforcement (e.g. FBI)
  • School / University
common themes for these emails

Account deactivation scare:

  • Adversary poses as a company whose service you use (e.g. Apple) and say that you have some number of hours to click a seemingly harmless link to verify this email address or your account will be deactivated.

Look-alike websites:

  • Adversary links to a fake website in the phishing email that looks identical to a real website. Visiting this website can secretly download a virus to your computer or steal other personal information about you.

Legal trouble:

  • Adversary poses as a form of law enforcement and warns the user if they do not click the link provided in the email then they could be in serious legal trouble (e.g. arrested, taken to court, etc).

Free "prize":

  • Adversary tells the user they won some sort of prize (e.g. gift card, money, iPhone, etc). The link to claim the prize leads to a malicious website that steals your name and address information where you want the "prize" shipped to.
  • **NOTE: If it's too good to be true then it's likely a scam.
why does attack work so well?

Emotional manipulation lets the adversary bypass the user's rational thinking skills:

Fear

  • Deactivation of accounts or worry of being in some kind of trouble leads the user to be fearful about an urgent situation. This pushes the user to stop everything and respond to the scam in an immediate manner leaving their critical thinking behind.

Urgency

  • Actions that must be taken within a limited amount of time lead the user to make irrational decisions such as clicking on links before thinking or replying to an email with their personal information enclosed.

Excitement

  • False promises of free gifts manipulate the user into thinking that there are only positives and no downsides to claiming the offer provided in the email.
example of an email phishing attempt

Say you have an Apple account and get an email from what appears to be Apple saying that your Apple ID has been locked for security reasons.

The email also states that if you do not respond within 12 hours then your Apple ID will be suspended and you will no longer have access to your account.

There is a big button in the middle of the page that says “Verify your Account”. If you were to click it, it would lead you to a webpage that looks just like Apple’s official webpage where it tells you to enter your email and password to verify the account.

Since this is a fake website instead of verifying your account it steals your Apple login information and gains access to your account.

**NOTE: If you have other accounts with the same email and password then the attacker has access to those as well. All they have to do is try that email and password combination on popular websites to see if you use the same login information.
See information about password security here.

why you should care

This type of attack is one of the most dangerous because users often fall for the social engineering tactics the adversary uses.

Your important information (e.g. account login and credit card information) is what the adversary is trying to gain access to.

If the adversary gains access to sensitive information like this then they can steal your money, make unauthorized purchases, or even cause identity theft.

If you work for a well-known or larger company then you may be at a higher risk for phishing attacks. This is because adversaries often target smaller networks of people within a larger company in order to steal information that the general public doesn’t have access to.

tools & tips

Check out these tips to avoid falling for email phishing traps.

check email address of the sender

account_box

It is extremely easy for adversaries to change the displayed name on a sent email, so never rely on that when verifying the legitimacy of an email. Always look at the email address it was sent from instead.

Does the sender's address have an official website signature (e.g. @company.org) or is there something off about it (e.g. @cmpany.org, @compeny.org, or @c0mpany.org)?

If the sender's official website signature is correct, double check the username before the "@" and make sure it isn't slightly misspelled.

beware of imposters

face

An attacker could gain access to the email account of one of your friends, family members, or coworkers and pose as them to get you to click on a malicious link.

If someone you know sends you a link to a "funny video" or something else that urgently needs to be clicked it is usually a good idea to check with them via phone call or text to make sure that they actually sent the link and it was not someone who gained access to their account.

who is the email addressed to?

person

Many phishing attempts will begin with “Dear customer” instead of “Dear insert your name here”.

When an official source is emailing you they have your name on file so they should always address you by your first name.

Some adversaries may have access to your first name, so just because an email is correctly addressed to you do not assume it is a legitimate email.

misspellings and bad grammar

assignment

If you receive an email from a seemingly official source then make sure that the email doesn't have any misspellings or bad grammar.

An easy way to check for this is to simply copy and paste any of the text that is not hyperlinked into the document editor of your choice to easily see where the grammatical errors lie.

think before you click

Take an extra moment to think before you click on any links contained in emails. If you are ever unsure it’s always good to ask someone else if they think a given email could be a phishing attempt.

When in doubt it's always better to go to the official website’s source instead of clicking on links in emails. For example if there is an email from a popular clothing brand about a sale, it is better to search up their website in your browser rather than clicking on the link provided in the email.

if it seems too good to be true then it is likely a scam

If you receive an email that you were "selected out of 1000 participants to win a new iPhone" or you have been "randomly selected to win $500", then you should take a step back and assess the situation.

It may seem tempting but do not click the link in the email. Doing so will put you at risk for getting your personal information stolen.

create filter for phishing emails

Depending on your email provider, some but not all phishing emails will get sent to your spam or junk folder before they get put in your inbox.
