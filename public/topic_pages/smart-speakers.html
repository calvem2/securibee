about

Smart speakers have become very popular home technology. They are speakers that use a "wake phrase" to activate voice recognition technology to play music, turn on/off devices in your home, set alarms, make calls, and more!

wwhat are examples of smart speakers?

Some popular examples are Amazon’s “Echo, Google’s “Google Home” and Apple’s “HomePod”.

what is a "wake phrase"?

A "wake phrase" is the phrase that the smart speaker listens for to initiate interaction and listen for a command.

The wake phrases for popular smart speakers are:

  • For the Amazon Echo: "Alexa," "Amazon", "Echo" "Hey, Alexa"
  • For the Google Home: "Okay, Google" or "Hey, Google"
  • For the Apple HomePod : "Okay, Siri" , "Hey, Siri"

sources: DigitalTrends.com

is the smart speaker always listening?

Why you should care

PMany of these smart speakers use generic wake words to activate their systems, such as “Hey Siri” or “Alexa” or “Hey Google” and by design, these smart speakers are ALWAYS listening (unless instructed otherwise) . Someone could potentially take advantage of this and command your smart speaker to make a purchase, or control devices in your home.

Tools & Tips

We gathered some tips and tools to help you recognize and protect yourself against unsafe links!

Tip #1: Never directly click on a hyperlink on a website you are unfamiliar with.

Instead, inspect the URL that the hyperlinked text or image leads to and make sure that it is the website you want to visit.

Use tools like ScanURL.net , a community sourced website thatasks users to input a url, a website name and a description of the user’s hesitations about the website and will report to users if it has been reported as phishing, potentially dangerous or suspicious in the past.

Tip #2: Check for mispelled organization names

Potential adversaries often misspell common organization website names to try and trick users into clicking on their links.

Examples

  • facbook instead of Facebook
  • am.azon instead of amazon
  • yuotube instead of youtube

Tip #3: Avoid pressing on advertisements that seem "too good to be true"

This is a trick adversaries use to lure people into clicking on their links. If you see an advertisement that looks cheaply made or unrealistic, do not press on it.

Examples

  • "$99 Rayban sunglasses for $20"
  • "Entire store 90% off!"
  • insert example

Tip #4: When downloading a file from a website, make sure that there are no duplicate download buttons.

Sometimes, advesaries insert images onto websites that LOOK like download buttons to trick users into downloading malicious files or to redirect them to websites they do not mean to visit. Double check that you are pressing on the correct download button for the file you wish yto download.
